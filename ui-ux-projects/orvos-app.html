Orvos is an app designed to alleviate stressors that are encountered by both patient and healthcare practitioner for a more streamlined experience.

Overview

Problem statement

How might we alleviate the stressors relating to filling out EMRs as well as patient experiences surrounding healthcare practices?

Our Solution

Inspired by one of our team members that has had numerous experiences as a patient within healthcare, we began to consider issues within healthcare and how we could provide a solution to one of these issues within 48 hours. Inspired by a podcast episode by Wireframe we decided to create an app that targets both patients and doctors as the primary demographic in order to create an overall better user experience for both professionals and patients all around.

Understanding the User

“21% of health professionals reported filing Electronic Health Records were a primary source of stress. 34% stated that this could be alleviated through an improvement of user friendliness.”

~ Reaction Data Article

SybridMed also details some pain points regarding the current system:

  • Without a program that supports specific sectors in healthcare (cardiologist vs dentist vs general practitioner) there is less support for the specific needs between each healthcare practice.
  • Training takes additional effort and personnel and requires additional time before the system is deployed and while it is deploying
  • Ineffective communication between the IT vendor of the EMR resulting in a lower ability to meet the needs of the users

As three of these points are each complex on their own, as a group we aimed to primarily target improving the interface and considering how to converge doctor-patient communication as well as other possibilities within the same application within a specific niche in healthcare. With our app specifically, we considered how such an app can be used at a hospital for regular patients.

Competitor Research

We identified different competitors and sorted them into different categories including appointment booking apps, apps for tracking health and appointments for personal use, apps for doctors to keep track of patients and other apps relating to logging medical identification. Other than referencing the general actions users could complete within the app (contacting doctors, storage of treatment plans + prescriptions, appointment reminders, data safety etc.) we also looked at any potential pain points or inefficiencies. This included how there were many separate apps for health tracking, appointment booking and personal use options, whereas there were no systems that worked together holistically. We also noted any user interface details whether the styles were compliant with accessibility as well as being simple to use.

Creating the System

Greyscale Wireframes

After figuring out our user flows we started designing the necessary pages on Figma. I was delegated the mobile appointment summary, and home page as well as the Patient EMR, test result page, and on the webapp. Here as a team, we also went through revising each others pages and tested the prototypes on Figma to ensure that the navigation and organisation between each page was simple and straightforward.

Takeaways

Hierarchy is your best friend

As healthcare documentation is complex and full of important details, we were also challenged to design for the ability to look at and document information. We were challenged with determining what was important information and determining the hierarchy.

Analyzing current systems is a great way to learn

Often design isn’t meant to reinvent the wheel but instead to improve the wheel. In our case we wanted to improve EMRs as well as provide a sort of EMR for the patient as well. Having been patients ourselves, we discussed the importance for patients to be more informed and have easier access to their health information, and at the same time, allowing both ends of the healthcare workflow to benefit from designed information systems.

Next Steps

As this project was more of an exercise, my group also acknowledged that we were touching on an area that undoubtedly requires more research and insight from users. However, connecting and staying in touch with user design in areas such as service industries has opened up our radar to want to eventually learn more and have learned the limitations of not having interdisciplinary knowledge and members to work with.
