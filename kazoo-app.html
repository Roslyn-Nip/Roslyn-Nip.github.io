Kazoo is an app that is designed to alleviate stressors related to childcare, streamlining the process of finding activities that are suitable for moderate supervision.

Overview

Problem statement

During the pandemic, there has been demand for resources to alleviate the stress resulting from the increased need to care for children.

Our Solution

Drawing from my groupmate’s experiences with childcare and the responsibilities surrounding the supervision of children, my group and I created Kazoo. Our an application tailors activities and content according to the age and interests of the child that is either using the app or for a guardian to choose on behalf of their child. The key goal is to alleviate stressors related to childcare, specifically finding activities that are suitable for moderate supervision.

I led the team and worked on user testing, UI/UX Design, visual design as well as some illustration and video editing. I mainly focused on the Home page, Search pages, and Settings pages. The culmination of our hard work and consideration of our users led to the creation of our app displayed below as well as our final Figma prototype here.

Understanding the User

“Being sequestered has caused a lot of emotional issues with my children. I have to work more jobs to support them financially yet figure out how to be home with them in order to make sure they’re fed, safe and educated.”

~ A woman, 50-64  (Courtesy of Pew Research Centre)

Ethnographic
Research (AEIOU)

First, my team conducted multiple rounds of research, first using the AEIOIU  (Activities, Environment, Interactions, Objects, and Users)method to create a framework to take notes on different aspects in their environment. With this method we conducted observational ethnographic research by either observing younger family members or looking at family vlogs and observing how childcare and keeping children busy is handled by different families.

In this we noticed that often in family settings, for younger children, often parents opt to have children be working on an activity that can be supervised while they multitask with other household chores. However, the parents often have to decide on activities to keep the children engaged and focused on the task. Sometimes the child even wished for a parent to be around with them.

As the Pew research centre collected statements relating to struggles during the pandemic, we first searched among these statements. We then settled upon one statement regarding how the care of their children may affect their lives and settled on the alleviation of childcare responsibilities.

Competitor Analysis

Then, we each chose 3 main competitors, TVO Kids, Youtube Kids, and Khan Academy Kids, to analyse using the principles of Usability Goals, User Experience Goals, and Usability Principles/ Heuristics. In each category, we selected 3-4 examples to rate and provide justification. We underwent this process in order to gain more context about what is being currently done in the field of education and entertainment for children in a safe and educational space.

Overall, we felt like there were some outdated graphics, issues with immersion, as well as less support for children’s creativity and parental guidance.

Scenarios

We created six different scenarios and thought about how this app might allow for a family, parent, or guardian might approach our application and what use cases it might fall within. Here we thought about how different family dynamics and compositions may alter the way the app is used and the reasons and motivations for potential users that may wish to use the app to streamline finding activities and things for children in their care.

Creating the System

Brainstorming

After understanding and going through various exercises to understand our users, we considered different options and directions, assembling a moodboard and considering design choices that would fulfil key words such as “friendly” “age-appropriate” and “engaging” were fulfilled with our design and user experience.

Tasks & Requirements

Based on the scenarios we constructed potential different use cases of our application. As a group we discussed and considered ways that user flows and the components of user flows can fulfil the needs. We created 12 rough task flows based on our identification of different needs presented by the different scenarios as an exercise to work out what may work best for our final user flows.

See full AEIOU documentation

User Flows

We created user flows to map out key flows we would like to focus on including:

  • Onboarding - so the app can take account of the age of the user
  • Checking bookmarks and history page - to find saved or revisiting activities
  • Navigating to settings - to change settings and also access the accessibility functions
  • Navigating to settings - to change settingsProfile viewing - to check and equip a badge and also access the accessibility functions

Wireframing

After moodbarding and discussing possible organization logic for each of our pages, we delegated different pages to lay out. We discussed our rough page designs, and referenced existing UI. We similar concepts such as the ability to tap on an unfamiliar word to find a definition similar to ereaders or ereading applications. Using our user flows as a guideline and editing flows when needed, we strove to ensure that things were fun yet still utilising familiar structures and interfaces found on other apps for easier learnability.

See full documentation for our wireframing process here.

User Testing

After wireframing, we developed our pages into high fidelity wireframes and refined them to be suitable for user testing. We conducted user tests on 7 participants, undergoing a formal consent form and generation of a testing scheme. This being our first user test, we focused our testing and questions on the ease of use, perception and expectations of their experience using the prototype, and suggestions for improvement. We found that user error was prevalant especially with the constraints of prototyping and confusion related to that, as well as familiarity with certain navigation styles affecting the routes that users may take to achieve the same task. Afterwards, we took note of our observations as well as what was said during the test and made changes to our app design accordingly.

Takeaways

After completing this lengthy process of creating an app from problem statement to full fledged prototype, I learned a lot about working with a group of designers. The most important skill I honed was working out a schedule and recognizing when things are behind or if there is extra time to squeeze in further refinement. I’m thankful to my group mates who were great sports when we decided to go beyond the extra mile to illustrate and create some custom card images as well as going back in and refining things after scrutinising our design at each stage. It isn’t often that I am able to have the opportunity to work with such a great group as well as for a longer time period. To wear different hats and take on the roles of a UI/UX designer, project manager, UX researcher and video editor all in one project really pushed me to learn and respect all these different roles in the process of creating an app. Special thanks to Dan Silvera for giving us great feedback at each stage!
